Dog poop is not just unsightly. It's also a health hazard.

And it's a growing problem in New York City.

It's become such a scourge that Council member Erik Botcher recently introduced a citywide public information campaign on LinkNYC machines and online.

On a recent walk around Morningside Heights I noticed a startling amount of poop on the sidewalks.

Particularly disheartening was the frequency with which those poops were within close proximity of a trash can.

A poop left by someone that is not a good neighbor.

I wanted to gauge the extent of the problem, so I took a walk around my neighborhood on a warm Sunday afternoon in July.

Armed with a map and pencil, I walked up and down every street from La Salle Street (124th) to 120th Street between Amsterdam Av. and Broadway, noting the location of every poop and trash can along the way.

The results were, well, disgusting.

I counted 54 poops in this relatively small corner of the neighborhood.

19 poops were counted on La Salle Street alone.

Is the problem a lack of trash cans?

The answer, sadly, is no.

I counted 27 trash cans in the same area.

So what can be done?

There are already laws in places that are designed to deter dog owners from leaving poop in public spaces.

NYC's 'curb your dog' fine was introduced in 1978. It carries a fine of $250 for anyone failing to remove dog poop.

Residents of Morningside Heights would be excused for wondering if it's time to try something new.

In the meantime, they'll be hoping dogwalkers heed the words of Botcher's campaign...
